History

  • Teacher contract expires. The AEA (Andover Education Association) and the School Committee meet four times during the duration of the summer, but a consensus is not reached.

  • The discussions have reached stalemate. As such, an impartial mediator from the state of Massachusetts is called in to control the discussion and debate.

  • The first mediation session between the School Committee and the AEA is held.

  • The teacher union hold a rally downtown, demanding a new contract.

  • The AEA and the School Committee meet twice during the summer. A contract has not been negotiated yet, meaning the teachers have gone a full year without it.

  • 57 weeks have passed since the teachers have had a contract. The two parties meet in separate rooms, with the mediator communicating between them.

  • Both sides express optimism about a contract being negotiated before the start of a new year. The school committee plans on modifying the block schedule to provide better continuity, in order to both reduce costs and be “responsive to student needs.”

  • The AEA submits a counter-proposal to the contract put forth by the School Committee. Another mediation session is expected.

  • The AEA votes to start Work-to-Rule on January 3rd, 2012, the day when school returns from Winter Break. Work-to-Rule means that teachers only do the duties required in their contracts, nothing more or less.

  • The discussions have reached stalemate. As such, an impartial mediator from the state of Massachusetts is called in to control the discussion and debate.

  • A task force is formed, consisting of six teachers and administrators, to study possible changes to the new schedule.

  • The School Committee and the AEA are still stalled, and the teachers continue to operate on the terms of their past contract. The MTA (Massachusetts Teacher Association) holds a rally with the AEA, calling for Union Solidarity.

  • A temporary contract is reached. Work-to-Rule ends. It is expected to be ratified April 2nd, 2012.

  • The negotiations fail. The High School Scheduling Committee was supposed to recommend a new schedule, but they failed to do so.

  • 66 “Pink Slips”, warning for layoffs, are distributed to teachers. This is because the town intends to save $500,000.

  • Talks halt as teachers wait for a report from the factfinder, the independent mediator, as they were expected to deliver on May 15th but were postponed due to an injury.

  • A group of 40 teachers attempt to prevent the reaccreditation of the school, as one teacher claims it is “the only leverage we have left at the bargaining table”. However, the school is reaccredited.

  • Teachers approve the new contract, following 96 weeks of discussion. It is in effect until August 31st, 2014. The teachers are now to teach 3 classes both semester. However, the mediator recommends that in the next school year that a new schedule is created. This is the 7+H Schedule.

  • The 7+H Schedule is revealed by the High School Scheduling Committee.

  • The School Committee votes unanimously in favor of the 7+H Schedule.

  • The AEA votes overwhelmingly in favor of the schedule. The schedule is set to debut in the school year of 2015-16.

  • Superintendent Marinel McGrath announces that the schedule will happen during the 2016-17 school year.

  • The schedule is stopped for 2016-17 due to Superintendent Sheldon Berman scrapping money from the budget to compromise with the Town Manager’s. Instead of the schedule, more elementary school teachers would be hired.

  • A survey finds that over half of the teachers oppose the new schedule.

  • Sheldon Berman reveals the new budget for the district, $79.8 million, a $3 million increase from the years before. The new schedule is slated to cost $513,000.

So where are we now? Currently, the budget has yet to be approved. There’s still a chance to repeal the schedule. Act now!
